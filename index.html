What I'm 😍 Right Now

Travel around Brooklyn, Manhattan, Queens, and Jersey City to eat 10 scoops of ice cream in one day to call yourself a Hillionaire Extraordinaire. The contest(?) ended on Labor Day, but I can't stop being a superfan since being invited to celebrate with the Ample Hills Founders.

I. Love. Magic. So when Malcom Moon gifted me free tickets to see magic on Broadway, I was elated. Derren Brown manages to weave a story line through all his tricks reminds me of this article about what makes a good stand-up comedy special-- and is maybe even more impressive because this is even less expected from a magic show. Brown's YouTube videos and Netflix specials are also good, but nothing beats live magic. Don't read anything about it first.

I've gotten up to 40% at places... it's way too good to be true. (Until it goes the way of MoviePass, use my referral code EMILY2131 and we both get an extra perk)

Gimlet Media's Reply All is the best podcast of all time. So good, in fact, that they have anticipated the need to create a playlist specifically for brand new listeners. Did it become even better when I found out that co-host PJ Vogt used to be roommates with Antoni Porowski during his pre-Queer Eye days? Obviously.

What I've 📖 & Recommend

I read The Magician's Assistant this summer and did not find it compelling enough to go to Ann Patchett's recent reading at the 92Y. Clearly, that was a mistake. The Dutch House is described as a "modern fairytale" in multiple reviews, which I don't exactly get, but it is an incredible story of family -- with multiple references to The Hungarian Pastry Shop.

I'd seen this book on lists since it came out last year but somehow it never made it on to my neverending NYPL Holds list until recently. I had assumed that the story took place in Louisiana (because of previous experiences at crawfish boils? I don't know), but it's set on the coast of North Carolina. The story of how the protagonist raised herself surrounded by nature was much more compelling to me than the parallel murder mystery--Owens is a clear nature expert with a BS in zoology and Ph.D. in animal behavior-- but together created a great read.

I have not stopped thinking about this book since I read it over the summer, but no one has taken my recommendation to read it yet because all I can tell them is that it's about trees. But it is about trees. And a handful of humans' relationships to trees. And each other. The thoughtfulness behind the entire book, it's commentary on climate change and the environment, and how everything comes together in the end is incredible. (Also it won the Pulitzer Prize.)

I did not give this book 5 stars on Goodreads, but I wanted to so badly. It's impossible for this fictionalized account of the factual sexual assaults among a Bolivian Mennonite community between 2005-2009 to be as gripping as the original story, but it's a thought-provoking attempt to imagine what the aftermath would look like for this terrorized community of women trying to reclaim their power.
